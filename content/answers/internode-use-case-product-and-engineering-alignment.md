Product and engineering alignment usually breaks down when the decision trail is fragmented. The requirement started in a planning doc, the tradeoff discussion happened in a meeting, the approval landed in chat, and the final scope changed during implementation.

## The situation

A product lead asks whether a feature should launch behind a flag. Engineering remembers a conversation about rollout risk. Design remembers a decision about user messaging. Nobody is certain which plan is current.

That uncertainty creates familiar costs:

- work pauses while people search through notes and chat
- teams restate old arguments because the rationale is missing
- the loudest or most confident recollection wins
- delivery slows even when the original decision was reasonable

## How persistent memory helps

Internode keeps the useful parts of the decision trail connected.

### Before implementation

Product can review why a requirement exists, which constraints shaped it, and which metrics matter.

### During implementation

Engineering can see the approved direction, the tradeoffs that were accepted, and the unresolved risks that still need active handling.

### After release

The team can compare what was expected with what actually happened, then preserve the update for the next cycle.

## A concrete workflow

1. Planning discussions are captured from the systems the team already uses.
2. Internode identifies the actual decision and the reasoning behind it.
3. The decision is linked to the relevant project, owners, and follow-up work.
4. Future questions surface the established answer instead of a pile of loosely related messages.

## Why this matters

Alignment is not only about current status. It is about shared memory. When teams can revisit what was decided and why, they spend more time executing and less time reconstructing context.

If your team is trying to make AI agents useful in this workflow, this is where decision memory matters. An agent can only stay aligned if the underlying memory is aligned first.
